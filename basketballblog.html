Coaching Basketball

In prior seasons I assisted other head coaches with practices and games, mostly keeping kids in line and keeping their focus on a drill. However I never took on, or felt the pressure of, any of the responsibilities that came along with the role. I was sheilded from a lot. This was an awakening for me at the start of this season. I never truly appreciated all the work that was involved. The planning that goes into teaching a concept. Running a successful practice. Evaluating player skillsets. Creating a rotation for your roster. It's not all pie in the sky and it's a highly visible responsibility. You are actually an inspiration for these children.

Head coach responsibilities include developing drills, developing player skills, and managing playing time with substitutions during a game. Other responsibilities that a head coach takes on are listed below.

  • Teach basic skills (dribbling, passing, shooting)
  • Organize team practices
  • Evaluate individual player skills to create a skill baseline
  • Evaluate team knowledge and develop team drills
  • Set parent expectations
  • Create a rotation with your roster
  • Give assignments to your assistant coach(es)
  • Set player expecations
  • Set parent expectations (did I mention that?)

It is noteworthy to mention that coaching youth basketball involves an extraordinaty amount of patience. More often than not, as I've experienced this year, the ideas you may have thought you could explain to a group of energetic kids may not always play out the way it was envisioned. Take for example a practice when I deciced to introduce a "boxing-out" routine. I had four surrounding the key, while I had another four on the outside perimeter as the offense. The goal was to have the four guarding the key to keep the outside four around the perimeter out of the key. When I shot the ball in the air, one of the kids on defense shoved an offensive player and a small fight started. There I had to referee a shoving match, explain to them that it's not about shoving, and calm down what was turning into a heated arguement. That 3 minute distraction spoiled the drill for the other six that were willing to listen because they got bored and wanted to do something else.

Good God.

So as you can see, it's not as easy as managing your player's playing time during a game. Nor is it about being that loud voice on the sideline. And it sure is not as easy as other experienced head coaches may make the job seem.
